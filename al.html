📝 AL 3: Tests & Assessment Principles

Achievement tests measure course objectives after instruction (final exams). Diagnostic tests identify learner needs before or during a course, such as pronunciation gaps. Placement tests assign students to appropriate levels, while aptitude tests (e.g., MLAT) predict future success but are rarely used today for communicative goals.

Core principles: practicality (cost, time, scoring efficiency), reliability (consistency across raters, tasks, and time), and validity (the test truly measures the intended construct). Types of validity include content-related, criterion-related, construct, consequential, and face validity. Authenticity ensures tasks mirror real-world language use (e.g., ordering food rather than decontextualized grammar). Washback describes how tests influence teaching and learning; positive washback occurs when formative feedback improves learner strategies. A well-designed test balances all five qualities.
⭐ Example: A speaking test using a job interview role-play (high authenticity & construct validity) vs. a multiple-choice grammar quiz that fails to measure oral interaction (low validity).
Reliability breakdown: student-related (anxiety/fatigue), rater reliability (inter- and intra-rater consistency), test administration (environmental factors), and test reliability (unambiguous items). For classroom teachers, criterion-referenced tests are more useful than norm-referenced because they link directly to lesson objectives and provide actionable feedback.
⚙️ AL 4: Instructional Design (ID)

Instructional design is a systematic procedure for creating effective, efficient, and engaging educational experiences. The ADDIE model (Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, Evaluation) ensures alignment among goals, strategies, and assessment.

Analysis involves needs assessment and performance gaps. Design specifies measurable learning objectives and selects media. Development produces materials (print, digital). Implementation delivers instruction in authentic settings. Evaluation includes formative (revisions during design) and summative (overall worth). ID is learner-centered — the focal point is learner performance, not teaching. It is goal-oriented, focused on real-world performance (congruence between learning and application environments). ID relies on data-driven decisions from start to finish, using subject-matter experts and iterative feedback. Language instruction via ID addresses four problems: selection/presentation of elements, knowledge base establishment, development of recall/deployment, and coherent course architecture.
📌 Example: Needs analysis reveals nurses need patient interaction English → design objectives (“explain symptoms”) → develop role-play cards & audio → implement in simulation lab → formative evaluation by peer feedback.
ID is typically a team effort involving designers, SMEs, media producers, and project managers. Even a single teacher benefits from systematic thinking to avoid incongruities among objectives, teaching, and tests.
🌍 AL 1: Applied Linguistics & Teacher Roles

Applied linguistics (for language teaching) requires a coherent theory of language, rigorous descriptive tools, and research directly linked to L2 pedagogy. It cannot rely on folk grammar or pure intuition.

The native vs. non-native teacher (NNEST) debate shifted with Medgyes (1994) questioning the assumption that native speakers are inherently superior. NNEST advantages include: acting as successful learning models, teaching effective strategies, anticipating difficulties, showing empathy, using shared L1 when beneficial, and familiarity with local curriculum. Teacher roles include: Controller (full control during accuracy stages), Organiser (managing environment, students, time, materials), Prompter (encouraging without taking over), Participant (joining activities but careful not to hinder monitoring), and Resource (adding value beyond internet, acting as catalyst). Effective teachers shift roles dynamically depending on activity stage (presentation, practice, production).
🧑‍🏫 Example: A non-native teacher says, “I used to confuse ‘borrow’ and ‘lend’ too — here’s the strategy I used” → builds rapport and strategy awareness.
The 3Ps model (Presentation, Practice, Production) guides lesson structure. Assessment and error correction vary by stage: immediate correction during accuracy, delayed or gentle correction during fluency work.
📊 AL 2: Assessment, Measurement & Evaluation

Assessment is the ongoing process of appraising student performance (incidental or planned). Testing is a subset — a structured instrument that measures ability, knowledge, or performance within a domain.

Measurement quantifies performance (e.g., 85% on a quiz), while evaluation interprets those numbers to make decisions (e.g., “needs review” or “proceed to next unit”). Informal assessment includes unplanned comments and coaching; it is nonjudgmental and embedded in daily teaching. Formal assessment is systematic, planned (midterms, finals). Formative assessment supports ongoing growth through feedback; it is forward-looking. Summative assessment summarizes learning at the end (final exams). Norm-referenced tests compare students to a peer group (percentile ranks). Criterion-referenced tests measure mastery of specific objectives (e.g., 80% to pass). For classroom teachers, criterion-referenced tests are more useful because they align directly with lesson goals and provide diagnostic feedback.
📈 Example: Weekly vocabulary quizzes with comments on usage = formative. Final cumulative exam = summative. A driving test where you must parallel park = criterion-referenced.
Effective assessment integrates both formal and informal methods, balances reliability and validity, and promotes positive washback. Evaluation always involves assigning worth and often leads to action (remediation, advancement, course redesign).

✨ Core synthesis: Validity & reliability are foundational for tests. ADDIE guides instructional design. NNESTs bring unique comparative advantages. Criterion-referenced, formative assessment is optimal for classroom decision-making.
